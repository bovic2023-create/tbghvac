Why Gas Furnace Diagnosis Starts at the Flue, Not the Thermostat<h2>
A gas furnace has two separate airstreams moving through it simultaneously. The combustion airstream draws in gas, burns it, produces heat and exhaust gases, and routes those gases out through the flue. The supply airstream (the air your family breathes) gets drawn in through the return, passes over the outside of the heat exchanger, picks up heat, and gets pushed through the ducts into your living space.
The heat exchanger is the wall between those two streams. It is a sealed metal chamber, and its integrity is everything. When a heat exchanger develops a stress fracture from repeated thermal expansion and contraction cycles, from overheating caused by restricted airflow, or from corrosion on an aging unit, combustion gases find a path into the supply air. Carbon monoxide, which has no color and no smell, travels through the duct system into the rooms your family is sleeping in.
The dangerous part is that a cracked heat exchanger doesn't always announce itself dramatically. The furnace keeps running. The heat keeps coming out. The CO detector may not trigger until concentrations build to a threshold level. Symptoms of low-level CO exposure, including headaches, fatigue, nausea, and a flu-like feeling that clears up when you leave the house, are easy to attribute to something else entirely.
This is why we don't start a gas furnace call in #State by checking the thermostat wiring.

Common Gas Furnace Failures in #State: What They Mean and What They Don't<h2>
Not every furnace problem is a heat exchanger problem. Most gas furnace failures fall into a smaller category of common faults, and most of them are repairs, not replacements.
A dirty or failed flame sensor is one of the most common causes of a furnace that ignites and then shuts back down within seconds. The flame sensor is a thin metal rod that sits inside the burner flame and generates a microamp signal, tiny but critical, telling the control board that combustion is happening. When it oxidizes or gets coated with residue, that signal drops below the readable threshold and the safety system cuts the gas. Cleaning or replacing a flame sensor is a simple, inexpensive repair.^^
A failed inducer motor prevents the furnace from starting at all. The inducer is a small blower that creates negative pressure in the combustion chamber before ignition, pulling fresh air in and establishing proper flue draft. A pressure switch monitors that draft and won't allow ignition until it confirms the inducer is producing the right pressure differential. When the inducer motor fails, the furnace runs through its startup sequence and stops without ever firing the burners.^^
A compromised secondary heat exchanger appears in high-efficiency condensing furnaces. These units use a secondary heat exchanger to extract additional heat from the flue gases before they exit, which means the secondary exchanger runs cooler, is exposed to acidic condensate, and can develop corrosion-driven cracks over time. When this happens, the furnace may display a fault code or simply begin underperforming without an obvious symptom.
The repair decision depends on the specific fault, the furnace's age, and the cost of that repair in context. We'll lay out all of your options.

How a Cold-Climate Heat Pump Actually Works in #State Winters<h2>
A heat pump doesn't create heat. It moves it. In summer, it works exactly like a central air conditioner, absorbing heat from the indoor air and releasing it outside. In winter, that process reverses: the system absorbs heat energy from the outdoor air and pumps it inside your home.
The physics that makes this possible is refrigerant thermodynamics. Refrigerant at low pressure evaporates at very low temperatures, cold enough to absorb heat energy even from frigid outdoor air. That vapor gets compressed, which raises its temperature dramatically, and then releases that heat indoors through the indoor coil. The outdoor air doesn't need to be warm. It just needs to contain more thermal energy than the refrigerant at low suction pressure.
Standard single-stage heat pumps lose heating capacity as outdoor temperatures drop because refrigerant density at suction pressure falls with temperature, reducing the mass flow through the compressor. Enhanced vapor injection addresses this directly by injecting additional refrigerant vapor at an intermediate pressure point in the compression cycle. This boosts mass flow and maintains compressor discharge temperature, which means the system keeps moving meaningful heat into your #State home even through the coldest nights your area typically sees.

What Actually Happens as an AC System Ages<h2>
Air conditioners don't fail randomly. There's a progression to how they decline, and understanding that progression helps you recognize where your system sits in it.
<list>
The compressor is the heart of the outdoor unit: a motor and pump assembly that pressurizes the refrigerant. Compressors are designed to last 15 to 20 years, but they get there by working hard. Every start-up cycle stresses the windings. High operating temperatures accelerate refrigerant oil breakdown. Contaminants including moisture, acid, and metallic debris from wear shorten compressor life progressively. When a compressor begins to fail, early signs often appear: high amp draw at startup, reduced system pressures, occasional hard starts that seem to clear on their own. By the time it seizes, those signs have typically been present for a season or two.^^
The evaporator coil sits inside your air handler and absorbs heat from the indoor air. Most are copper-aluminum construction and operate under refrigerant pressure. Over time, particularly in systems 12 to 18 years old, formicary corrosion develops. This is a chemical reaction between formic acid in the indoor air and the copper tubing, producing pinhole leaks that allow refrigerant to escape slowly. Cooling capacity drops. Charge drops. If the system uses R-22, the refrigerant that was phased out of production in 2020, recharging becomes increasingly expensive as remaining supplies dwindle.^^
The refrigerant type in your existing system matters significantly for replacement buyers in #State. R-22 systems with active leaks are poor repair targets: the refrigerant cost is prohibitive and rising. Systems manufactured between 2010 and 2023 typically use R-410A, which is currently under EPA phasedown. New systems being installed in #State are transitioning to R-32 and R-454B, refrigerants with substantially lower environmental impact. Knowing what refrigerant a new system uses affects your long-term service cost picture.
