Gas Furnace Repair: Accuracy and Safety on the Same Call<h2>
Gas furnaces are mechanically straightforward but chemically significant. When the system works correctly, the heat exchanger (the sealed metal chamber that contains combustion gases) keeps carbon monoxide and nitrogen oxides away from the air your family breathes. When something goes wrong with that separation, the consequences can be serious.
Every gas furnace call we run includes electronic combustion analysis. We insert a flue gas probe and read carbon monoxide output in parts per million, oxygen content, and stack temperature. Those numbers tell us what a visual inspection cannot: whether the furnace is combusting cleanly and whether the heat exchanger is doing its job. If the readings are off, we show you the data on the analyzer screen, explain what it means, and walk through your real options. Repair, heat exchanger replacement, or full system replacement. We give you honest context for each.
We will never recommend a new furnace on a system that can be responsibly repaired.

Heat Pump Installation: One System for Heating and Cooling<h2>
A heat pump does what an air conditioner does, plus heating. It moves heat from outdoors to indoors in winter, and indoors to outdoors in summer, using the same refrigerant cycle reversed. Modern cold-climate heat pumps, engineered with enhanced vapor injection (EVI) compressor technology, can extract usable heat from outdoor air down to negative 13°F. That's a performance level older heat pump designs couldn't approach.
If you're replacing an aging gas furnace, an aging central AC, or both at once, a heat pump installation is worth a serious conversation. One system handles both functions, simplifying your mechanical room and reducing the number of components that can age out on you.
Every heat pump we install begins with a Manual J load calculation: a formal analysis of your home's actual heating and cooling load based on square footage, insulation levels, window area, ceiling height, and local climate data. This determines the correct system capacity. An oversized unit short-cycles and creates humidity problems. An undersized unit runs constantly and never catches up. Load calculation is not optional for us.

Air Conditioner Replacement: The Right Decision, Made With Real Information<h2>
An air conditioner replacement is a significant purchase. The range runs from roughly $5,000 to $12,000 or more depending on system size, efficiency rating, and installation complexity. Making that decision well requires accurate information, not pressure.
We help homeowners work through the repair-vs-replace question with a structured framework. Systems under 10 years old with a diagnosable component failure almost always make more sense to repair. Systems over 15 years old facing major failures, compressor, heat exchanger, or evaporator coil, usually tip toward replacement. Systems over 18 years old are running on borrowed time regardless of the current symptom.
All new residential AC systems carry SEER2 efficiency ratings, a more rigorous testing standard than the old SEER system it replaced in 2023. A modern SEER2 16 system delivers meaningfully more efficiency than a legacy SEER 10 unit. We'll walk you through the numbers specific to your home and usage so you're making a confident decision, not a panicked one.

What actually happens during an HVAC service call, and what does the tech do when they arrive? When a technician arrives, the first step is always a quick safety check: listening to the system, noting any visual anomalies, checking for gas odor on furnace calls. From there, the diagnostic process is systematic. How much does HVAC repair typically cost? Repair costs vary with the nature of the failure, the system type, and the parts involved. A capacitor replacement on a central AC unit costs far less than a compressor swap or a refrigerant circuit repair. We provide a clear, written diagnosis and price before any repair begins. Should I repair my current system or put that money toward a replacement? The honest answer depends on your system's age, the nature of the failure, and what repair costs relative to replacement. For systems under 10 years old, a diagnosable fault almost always justifies repair. For systems over 15 years old facing major component failures, compressor, heat exchanger, or evaporator coil, replacement typically delivers better long-term value. We'll give you both options with accurate pricing before you decide anything. The right call depends on your specific situation, and we'll tell you what the data supports rather than what generates the larger job.^^
What is combustion analysis, and should I ask for it on a gas furnace call? Combustion analysis measures flue gas chemistry with an electronic analyzer, reading carbon monoxide output in parts per million, oxygen content, and stack temperature directly from the furnace exhaust. These measurements tell us, with actual data, whether the furnace is burning cleanly and whether the heat exchanger is keeping combustion gases out of your supply air. A clean-burning furnace produces very low flue CO, generally under 50 parts per million. Readings above 200 PPM warrant immediate follow-up. You should ask for combustion analysis on every gas furnace service call. It's the diagnostic standard that separates a professional gas appliance technician from someone checking thermostat wiring and moving on.
