What #State's Housing Stock Means for Your HVAC System<h2>
#State has one of the most varied residential housing landscapes in the country. Older neighborhoods, the ones built before the mid-1980s, were often constructed when central air conditioning was a retrofit addition, not a design standard. The ductwork was threaded through existing framing to accommodate a system the house wasn't originally built around. That means return air pathways are frequently undersized, supply runs are longer than ideal, and static pressure across the air handler is higher than modern equipment is rated for.
When a high-efficiency variable-speed system gets installed in an older #State home with undersized return ducts, the blower works against restriction constantly. The heat exchanger or evaporator coil gets starved of airflow. Components run hotter and harder than they should. The homeowner sees higher bills and lower comfort, but no obvious malfunction. Just a system that never quite performs the way the sales sheet said it would.

Newer #State Construction and the Oversizing Problem<h2>
On the other end of the spectrum, rapid suburban expansion across #State over the past two decades has produced a generation of homes where HVAC systems were sized by builders using square footage rules rather than formal load calculations. Builders tend to oversize for a simple reason: a system that's too large will always reach the setpoint, which means no complaints at move-in.
The problem surfaces over time. An oversized AC runs in short bursts. It drops the air temperature quickly, then shuts off before it has time to pull humidity out of the air. In the humid months that #State homeowners know well, this creates a clammy, uncomfortable indoor environment even when the thermostat reads the right number. Short-cycling also stresses the compressor, which is built to run at steady state, not rapid start-stop cycles that spike electrical draw every few minutes.
When we're called to service a newer #State home, we check the system's actual runtime patterns. If the unit is meaningfully oversized for the real load, that context shapes every recommendation we make going forward.

Gas Furnace Repair in #State: Safety Is the First Diagnostic Step<h2>
Homeowners in #State who heat with natural gas are relying on a combustion appliance, and combustion appliances require a different diagnostic standard than purely mechanical systems. When something goes wrong with a gas furnace in a #State home, our first priority is the flue gas, not the thermostat wiring.
Our technicians carry electronic combustion analyzers on every gas furnace call in #State. The analyzer measures carbon monoxide output in parts per million, oxygen content in the flue, and stack temperature. These readings tell us, with actual measured data, whether the heat exchanger is intact and whether the furnace is burning cleanly. A CO reading below 50 PPM in the flue is normal. Readings above 200 PPM warrant immediate follow-up. Readings that rise when the blower kicks on, indicating combustion gases are entering the supply air, tell us the heat exchanger has been compromised and the furnace goes off until the problem is resolved.
This is not a test most HVAC companies run on a standard service call in #State. We run it because it's the only way to know what's actually happening inside the combustion chamber.

Heat Pump Installation Across #State: System Design First<h2>
Heat pump installation in #State requires attention to the actual climate zone where the home sits. #State encompasses regions with meaningfully different winter design temperatures, and the right heat pump specification varies accordingly. A standard single-stage heat pump may perform adequately in the milder parts of #State. In areas of #State that see sustained temperatures below 30°F, a cold-climate heat pump with EVI compressor technology, rated to operate at negative 13°F, is the responsible specification.
EVI allows the compressor to inject additional refrigerant vapor mid-cycle, maintaining heating output at outdoor temperatures that would cause a standard heat pump to lose capacity rapidly. Cold-climate models are not niche products anymore. They're the correct default for reliable heat pump performance through most of #State's realistic heating season.
